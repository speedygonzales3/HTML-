Saving the Humpback Whale from Extinction

The verge of Extinction

New study suggests that the humpback whale are only 25,000 away from reaching their old population

It is estimated that there were once 27,000 of these distinctive whales which swam freely around the southwest Atlantic

Why did humpback whales almost go extinct?

Overhunting was the main cause of their endangerment.
Companies used to hunt whales to sell things that come from them, like meat, oil, and blubber.

Humpback whales are migratory animals, which means they move around depending on the season.
This group of mammals prefer to spend their summers
close to southwest Antartic and Antartic waters, where there is plenty of food for them to eat.

In the winter they return to warmer waters towards the coast of Brazil to breed and give birth.
As the whales travelled down south for their feed,
they were brought into contact with the industrial fishery based in South Georgia from 1904.

Thus, humpback whales were hunted by the fishery and as a result their population quickly fell
and they became extremely rare by the late 1920s.
